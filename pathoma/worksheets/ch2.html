I

Acute Inflammation

High Yield

Acute inflammation is the immediate, innate-immunity response to infection or tissue necrosis. It permits neutrophils, plasma proteins, and fluid to leave postcapillary venules and enter damaged tissue. Its histologic signature is edema + neutrophils.

Mediators of Acute Inflammation

Toll-like receptors (TLRs)

Sit on macrophages, dendritic cells, and lymphocytes. They recognize PAMPs (pathogen-associated molecular patterns) — conserved microbial molecules. The classic example: CD14 is a co-receptor that lets TLR4 recognize LPS on gram-negative bacteria. TLR engagement activates NF-κB, which turns on transcription of inflammatory cytokines.

Arachidonic acid metabolites

Phospholipase A₂ liberates arachidonic acid from the membrane. Two enzymes process it:

  • Cyclooxygenase (COX) → prostaglandins. PGI₂, PGD₂, PGE₂ drive vasodilation and ↑ vascular permeability. PGE₂ additionally mediates pain and fever.
  • 5-Lipoxygenase (LOX) → leukotrienes. LTB₄ attracts and activates neutrophils. LTC₄, LTD₄, LTE₄ (SRS-A) cause vasoconstriction, bronchospasm, ↑ permeability.
Mnemonic — "Neutrophils arrive for Breakfast" LTB₄ is the chemotactic leukotriene (B for Bring neutrophils). The others (C/D/E) do smooth muscle.

Mast cells

Distributed through connective tissue. Three activators: (1) trauma, (2) complement C3a / C5a (anaphylatoxins), (3) cross-linking of surface IgE by antigen. Immediate response → preformed histamine release (vasodilation, ↑ permeability). Delayed response → production of leukotrienes.

Complement

Circulating proinflammatory proteins activated through three pathways converging at C3 convertase:

  • Classical — C1 binds IgG or IgM on antigen ("GM makes classic cars")
  • Alternative — microbial surfaces directly activate C3
  • Lectin (MBL) — mannose-binding lectin binds sugars on microbes

Key products: C3a and C5a trigger mast cells (anaphylatoxins). C5a is also a neutrophil chemotactic. C3b is the big opsonin. MAC (C5b–C9) drills holes in membranes.

Buzzword — C5a If a vignette mentions a protein that is both a neutrophil chemotactic and triggers mast cell degranulation, think C5a. C5a and LTB₄ are the two classic chemotactics for neutrophils (plus IL-8 and bacterial products).

Hageman factor (Factor XII)

Inactive plasma protein made by the liver. Activated by contact with subendothelial/tissue collagen. Triggers four cascades: coagulation, fibrinolysis, complement, and the kinin system. Kinin cleaves HMWK to bradykinin → vasodilation, ↑ permeability, pain (analogous to histamine, but also painful).

Neutrophil Arrival — the 7 Steps

Figure 2.1 — The six vascular/cellular steps of the neutrophil cascade, with the resolution step omitted for clarity.

Step 1 — Margination

Vasodilation slows blood in postcapillary venules. Cells shift from the center of flow (axial stream) to the vessel periphery.

Step 2 — Rolling (Selectins)

Endothelial cells express selectins ("speed bumps"):

  • P-selectin — stored preformed in Weibel-Palade bodies, released by histamine
  • E-selectin — induced by TNF and IL-1

Both bind sialyl-Lewis X on leukocytes → loose, reversible attachment → rolling.

Step 3 — Adhesion (Integrins + CAMs)

TNF and IL-1 upregulate ICAM and VCAM on endothelium. C5a and LTB₄ upregulate integrins on leukocytes (and activate them to a high-affinity state). Integrin–CAM engagement = firm adhesion.

Clinical — Leukocyte Adhesion Deficiency (LAD-1) Autosomal recessive defect of integrins (CD18 β₂ subunit). Presents with delayed umbilical cord separation (> 30 days), neutrophilia (cells can't marginate, so they stay in the blood), and recurrent bacterial infections without pus. The failure of pus formation is the board giveaway.

Step 4 — Transmigration & Chemotaxis

Neutrophils diapedese between endothelial cells and follow chemoattractants: bacterial products (fMet-Leu-Phe), IL-8, C5a, LTB₄.

Step 5 — Phagocytosis

Pseudopods engulf microbes → phagosome → fuse with lysosome → phagolysosome. Enhanced by opsonins: IgG (Fc) and C3b.

Clinical — Chediak-Higashi Syndrome Autosomal recessive defect in LYST gene → protein trafficking defect → impaired phagolysosome fusion. Pentad: recurrent pyogenic infections, neutropenia (intramedullary death), giant granules in leukocytes, bleeding (dense granule defect), partial albinism, peripheral neuropathy.

Step 6 — Intracellular Killing

O₂-dependent killing (most effective) generates HOCl (bleach) in the phagolysosome:

  1. NADPH oxidase: O₂ → O₂⁻ (oxidative burst)
  2. SOD: O₂⁻ → H₂O₂
  3. Myeloperoxidase: H₂O₂ + Cl⁻ → HOCl
Two defects to distinguish CGD (chronic granulomatous disease) — NADPH oxidase defect (X-linked or AR). Recurrent infections with catalase⁺ organisms (S. aureus, Pseudomonas cepacia/Burkholderia, Serratia, Nocardia, Aspergillus) + granulomas. Screen: NBT test fails to turn blue; modern test DHR. MPO deficiency — usually asymptomatic, ↑ Candida. NBT normal (respiratory burst intact — just can't convert H₂O₂ → HOCl).

Step 7 — Resolution

Neutrophils undergo apoptosis within ~24 hours after stimulus clears.

Macrophages — Day 2–3

Derived from circulating monocytes; arrive via margination → rolling → adhesion → transmigration (same cascade). They ingest via phagocytosis and destroy via O₂-independent killing (lysozyme). Macrophages choose the next step of inflammation:

  • Resolution & healing — IL-10, TGF-β (anti-inflammatory)
  • Continued acute inflammation — IL-8 recruits more neutrophils
  • Abscess — acute inflammation walled off by fibrosis (macrophage-driven)
  • Chronic inflammation — antigen presentation to CD4⁺ T cells → cytokine loop
II

Chronic Inflammation

High Yield

Chronic inflammation is characterized by lymphocytes and plasma cells in tissue — a delayed but antigen-specific (adaptive immunity) response. Common triggers: persistent infection, viruses/mycobacteria/parasites/fungi, autoimmune disease, foreign material, some cancers.

T Lymphocytes

Produced in marrow → mature in thymus, where TCR rearranges and T cells commit to CD4⁺ helper or CD8⁺ cytotoxic lineages. T-cell activation always needs two signals:

CD4⁺ Helper T-cell activation

  1. Signal 1: extracellular antigen processed by APC, presented on MHC II, recognized by TCR
  2. Signal 2: B7 on APC binds CD28 on T cell

Two subsets:

  • Th1 — secretes IFN-γ: activates macrophages, drives IgG class switching, promotes Th1, inhibits Th2
  • Th2 — secretes IL-4, IL-5, IL-13: IgE class switching (IL-4), eosinophil activation + IgA switching (IL-5)

CD8⁺ Cytotoxic T-cell activation

  1. Signal 1: intracellular antigen presented on MHC I (all nucleated cells + platelets)
  2. Signal 2: IL-2 from an activated Th1

Kills via (1) perforin + granzyme and (2) FasL–Fas (both trigger target-cell apoptosis).

B Lymphocytes

Mature in marrow into naive B cells expressing surface IgM and IgD. Activation routes:

  1. Direct antigen binding → plasma cell secreting IgM/IgD (T-independent)
  2. T-dependent: B cell presents antigen on MHC II to CD4⁺ helper T cell; CD40 on B cell binds CD40L on T cell (signal 2) → T cell releases IL-4 and IL-5 → isotype class switching, somatic hypermutation, plasma cell maturation
Mnemonic — MHC partners MHC II → CD4 (2 × 4 = 8). MHC I → CD8 (1 × 8 = 8). Both equal 8.

Granulomatous Inflammation

A subtype of chronic inflammation. A granuloma is a collection of epithelioid histiocytes (macrophages with abundant pink cytoplasm), usually ringed by giant cells and lymphocytes.

TypeCentral NecrosisClassic Causes
CaseatingYes (cottage-cheese caseum)TB, fungal infections (Histo, Blasto, Cocci, Paracocci)
NoncaseatingNoSarcoidosis, Crohn disease, beryllium, cat-scratch, foreign body reaction

Steps of granuloma formation

  1. Macrophage processes antigen → presents on MHC II to CD4⁺ T cell
  2. Macrophage secretes IL-12 → CD4⁺ T cell polarizes to Th1
  3. Th1 secretes IFN-γ → converts macrophages into epithelioid histiocytes and giant cells
Board clue A patient on anti-TNF-α therapy (e.g., infliximab, etanercept) who presents with reactivation of TB — TNF-α maintains granuloma integrity. Always PPD-screen before starting anti-TNF.
III

Primary Immunodeficiency

High Yield

DiGeorge Syndrome

Developmental failure of the 3rd and 4th pharyngeal pouches due to 22q11.2 microdeletion. Results:

  • No thymus → T-cell deficiency
  • No parathyroids → hypocalcemia (tetany)
  • Conotruncal cardiac defects (tetralogy of Fallot, truncus arteriosus), abnormal facies, cleft palate
Mnemonic — CATCH-22 Cardiac · Abnormal facies · Thymic aplasia · Cleft palate · Hypocalcemia · 22q11.2

Severe Combined Immunodeficiency (SCID)

Defective cell-mediated AND humoral immunity. Three classic etiologies:

  1. Cytokine-receptor defect (most common; often X-linked, IL-2Rγ/common γ chain)
  2. Adenosine deaminase (ADA) deficiency — deoxyadenosine buildup is toxic to lymphocytes
  3. MHC class II deficiency — no CD4⁺ T-cell activation

Presents with susceptibility to fungal, viral, bacterial, and protozoal infections + opportunistic organisms + fatal reactions to live vaccines. Treatment: sterile isolation ("bubble baby") → stem cell transplant.

X-linked (Bruton) Agammaglobulinemia

Mutation in BTK (Bruton tyrosine kinase) → pre- and pro-B cells can't mature → no immunoglobulin. Boys present after 6 months of life (once maternal IgG wanes) with recurrent bacterial, enterovirus (polio, coxsackie), and Giardia infections. Live vaccines (polio) contraindicated.

Common Variable Immunodeficiency (CVID)

Low Ig from B-cell or helper T-cell defects. Presents in late childhood or later. ↑ risk of autoimmune disease and lymphoma.

IgA Deficiency

Low serum and mucosal IgA. Most common primary immunodeficiency (1 in 600). Most patients are asymptomatic. Classic risks: mucosal infections (especially viral), anaphylaxis to blood products (they contain IgA → recipient makes anti-IgA), and association with celiac (false-negative tTG IgA).

Hyper-IgM Syndrome

Mutated CD40L (on helper T cells, X-linked) or CD40 (on B cells, AR) → second signal can't be delivered → no class switching. Result: ↑ IgM, ↓ IgG/IgA/IgE → recurrent pyogenic infections (especially mucosal) due to poor opsonization.

Wiskott-Aldrich Syndrome

Mutation in WASP gene, X-linked. Triad:

Mnemonic — WAITER Wiskott-Aldrich · Immunodeficiency · Thrombocytopenia · Eczema · Recurrent infections. Bleeding is a major cause of death.

Complement Deficiencies

  • C5–C9 (MAC) deficiency — recurrent Neisseria infections (gonorrhoeae, meningitidis)
  • C1 esterase inhibitor deficiency — hereditary angioedema: periorbital and mucosal edema. ACE inhibitors contraindicated (they further ↑ bradykinin).
  • Decay accelerating factor (DAF/CD55) deficiency — paroxysmal nocturnal hemoglobinuria (MAC lysis of complement-unprotected RBCs)
  • Early (C1q/C4/C2) deficiency — associated with SLE (failure to clear immune complexes)
IV

Autoimmune Disorders

High Yield

Autoimmune disease reflects loss of self-tolerance: immune destruction of one's own tissues. Predominantly affects women of childbearing age (estrogen may reduce apoptosis of self-reactive B cells). Typical triggers: environmental exposure in genetically susceptible people (HLA associations, PTPN22 polymorphisms). Mechanisms include bystander activation, molecular mimicry, and epitope spreading (which explains overlap syndromes and relapsing-remitting course).

Tolerance breakdowns AIRE mutation → autoimmune polyendocrine syndrome (central tolerance failure). FoxP3 mutation → IPEX (Immune dysregulation, Polyendocrinopathy, Enteropathy, X-linked). Fas pathway mutation → ALPS (autoimmune lymphoproliferative syndrome).

Systemic Lupus Erythematosus (SLE)

Chronic, systemic. Classically a young African-American or Hispanic woman. Mechanism: antigen-antibody (type III hypersensitivity) complexes damage tissues; poorly cleared apoptotic debris (UV-damaged nuclei) drives self-reactive lymphocytes. Early complement deficiency (C1q/C4/C2) predisposes because immune complexes aren't cleared.

Diagnostic criteria — "SOAP BRAIN MD" Serositis · Oral ulcers · Arthritis · Photosensitivity · Blood (cytopenias) · Renal (diffuse proliferative GN) · ANA · Immunologic (anti-dsDNA, anti-Sm) · Neurologic (psychosis, seizures) · Malar rash · Discoid rash

Antibodies

  • ANA — sensitive, not specific
  • Anti-dsDNA & anti-Smith — highly specific for SLE; anti-dsDNA also correlates with renal disease severity
  • Anti-histone — drug-induced lupus (procainamide, hydralazine, isoniazid)

Libman-Sacks endocarditis

Sterile, small vegetations on both sides of the mitral valve (unlike bacterial endocarditis, which typically affects one side).

Antiphospholipid Antibody Syndrome (APS)

Autoantibodies to phospholipid-binding proteins. Three classic antibodies:

  • Anticardiolipin — causes false-positive VDRL/RPR (syphilis screens)
  • Anti-β₂-glycoprotein I
  • Lupus anticoagulant — paradoxically causes falsely elevated PTT in vitro but thrombosis in vivo

Clinically → arterial + venous thrombosis: DVT, stroke, hepatic vein thrombosis, recurrent pregnancy loss (placental thrombosis). Treatment: lifelong anticoagulation.

Sjögren Syndrome

Lymphocyte-mediated (type IV) destruction of lacrimal and salivary glands. Classic older woman with dry eyes (keratoconjunctivitis sicca), dry mouth (xerostomia), recurrent dental caries. Biopsy of minor salivary glands (lip) shows lymphocytic sialadenitis.

  • Anti-SSA (anti-Ro) and anti-SSB (anti-La)
  • Anti-SSA in a pregnant woman → risk of neonatal lupus + congenital heart block
  • ↑ risk of B-cell (MALT / marginal zone) lymphoma — new unilateral parotid enlargement late in the course

Systemic Sclerosis (Scleroderma)

Sclerosis of skin and visceral organs from fibroblast over-activation. Endothelial injury → ↑ endothelin, ↓ NO, ↑ TGF-β & PDGF → perivascular fibrosis. Two flavors:

Limited (CREST)Diffuse
SkinHands, face onlyDiffuse
OrgansLate visceral involvementEarly: lungs (fibrosis, PH), GI, kidneys (scleroderma renal crisis)
AntibodyAnti-centromereAnti-Scl-70 (anti-DNA topoisomerase I)
Mnemonic — CREST Calcinosis + anti-Centromere · Raynaud · Esophageal dysmotility · Sclerodactyly · Telangiectasias

Mixed Connective Tissue Disease (MCTD)

Overlap of features of SLE, scleroderma, and polymyositis. Anti-U1 RNP antibody is characteristic.

V

Wound Healing

High Yield

Healing starts when inflammation starts. It is a combination of regeneration (replacement with native tissue) and repair (replacement with fibrous scar).

Regeneration — the Three Tissue Classes

ClassCell cycle behaviorExamples
LabileContinuously dividing stem cellsSmall/large bowel (mucosal crypts), skin (basal layer), bone marrow
StableQuiescent in G₀; re-enter cycle on stimulusHepatocytes (liver regenerates by compensatory hyperplasia after partial resection), PCT, smooth muscle
PermanentCannot divide; hypertrophy onlyMyocardium, skeletal muscle, neurons — heal with scar

Repair — Scar Formation

Repair replaces damaged tissue with fibrous scar. Occurs when regenerative stem cells are lost (deep skin cut) or in permanent tissues.

Granulation tissue (initial phase)

  • Fibroblasts — deposit type III collagen (pliable)
  • Capillaries — bring nutrients, exchange gases
  • Myofibroblasts — contract and close the wound

Scar (late phase)

Type III collagen is replaced by stronger type I collagen.

Critical cofactors Vitamin C — hydroxylation of proline/lysine on procollagen (enables cross-links). Copper — lysyl oxidase cross-linking. Zinc — cofactor for collagenase, which removes type III to allow type I deposition. Deficiency of any → delayed wound healing.

Mediators of Healing

Growth factors act through receptor–ligand interactions (e.g., EGF). Key players:

  • TGF-α — epithelial and fibroblast growth factor
  • TGF-β — major fibroblast growth factor; also anti-inflammatory
  • PDGF — endothelium, smooth muscle, fibroblasts
  • FGF — angiogenesis, skeletal development
  • VEGF — angiogenesis

Cutaneous Healing — Primary vs. Secondary Intention

PrimarySecondary
Wound edgesApproximated (sutured)Left open — granulation tissue fills defect; myofibroblast contraction
ScarringMinimalMore substantial; wound contraction

Aberrant Healing

Delayed wound healing

  • Infection — most common cause; S. aureus is the most common culprit
  • Nutritional deficiency — vitamin C, copper, zinc
  • Foreign body, ischemia, diabetes, malnutrition

Dehiscence

Mechanical rupture of a wound — classically after abdominal surgery.

"Except" Rules — Don't Fall For These

  • Neutrophils arrive first — peak at 24 hr, clear by 24 hr post-resolution. Macrophages peak day 2–3
  • MHC I → CD8; MHC II → CD4 — multiply for 8 in both cases
  • Bruton agammaglobulinemia presents after 6 months because maternal IgG is protective earlier
  • SCID needs live-vaccine avoidance — any immunodeficiency with T-cell involvement should not receive live vaccines
  • Hereditary angioedema — avoid ACE inhibitors (further ↑ bradykinin)
  • Lupus anticoagulant is NOT an anticoagulant in vivo — it causes thrombosis (artifact in PTT assay only)
  • Anti-dsDNA tracks SLE renal activity; anti-Sm does not
  • Sarcoidosis = noncaseating, TB = caseating — but both are granulomatous
  • Anti-TNF-α therapy requires PPD screening first — TNF maintains granulomas
  • Keloid grows beyond wound margins; hypertrophic scar stays within
  • Type III collagen is early (pliable); type I is final (strong)
  • Vitamin C deficiency = scurvy: failure of proline/lysine hydroxylation → weak collagen
