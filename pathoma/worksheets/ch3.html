I

Basic Principles & Nomenclature

High Yield

A neoplasm is new tissue growth that is unregulated, irreversible, and monoclonal. These three features separate neoplasia from both hyperplasia (regulated, reversible, polyclonal) and repair.

Monoclonality — How We Prove It

Monoclonal = all tumor cells are derived from a single mother cell. Two classic assays:

  • G6PD isoforms. In females, lyonization (random X-inactivation) creates a 1:1 mosaic of G6PD-A and G6PD-B. Normal or hyperplastic tissue: 1:1. Neoplasia: only one isoform (all cells descend from a single progenitor whose active X was fixed).
  • B-cell light-chain ratio. Each B cell makes either κ or λ — never both. Normal κ:λ = 3:1. In hyperplasia (reactive lymphadenitis), the 3:1 ratio is preserved. In lymphoma, the ratio distorts (> 6:1 or inverted to κ:λ ≈ 1:3).
Board clue — κ:λ ratio A gastric MALT biopsy shows a B-cell infiltrate; flow shows κ:λ ratio of 9:1 → the population is clonal → lymphoma, not reactive inflammation.

Benign vs. Malignant — The Core Distinction

Benign tumors stay put. Malignant tumors invade locally and have the potential to metastasize. Only biopsy/excision definitively classifies a tumor — some benign tumors grow aggressively, some malignant ones grow slowly.

Epidemiology — The Big Three

  • Cancer = #2 cause of death in adults (after cardiovascular) and in children (after accidents).
  • Adult cancers by incidence: breast/prostate > lung > colorectal.
  • Adult cancers by mortality: lung > breast/prostate > colorectal. (Lung kills more because it's caught late.)

Screening

A cancer undergoes ~30 divisions before it becomes clinically detectable. Screening catches dysplasia (precancer) or early carcinoma before symptoms arise — but only counts as "effective" if it reduces cancer-specific mortality. The classic Step 1 screens:

  • Pap smear — cervical dysplasia (CIN) → cervical carcinoma
  • Mammography — DCIS or early invasive breast cancer
  • PSA + DRE — prostate cancer
  • Colonoscopy / FIT — colonic adenoma → colorectal cancer

Carcinogenesis

High Yield

Cancer starts with damage to the DNA of stem cells that overcomes repair but doesn't kill. Accumulated mutations disrupt proto-oncogenes, tumor suppressors, and regulators of apoptosis → growth and spread.

Radiation

Ionizing radiation

Nuclear accidents, radiotherapy. Generates hydroxyl free radicals that damage DNA. Associated cancers: AML, CML, and papillary thyroid carcinoma.

Nonionizing UV (UVB sunlight)

Forms pyrimidine dimers in DNA. Normally repaired by nucleotide excision repair (NER). Defective NER = xeroderma pigmentosum → extreme photosensitivity and multiple skin cancers at a young age. UV-associated cancers: basal cell carcinoma, SCC, melanoma.

RAS — how a point mutation creates an oncogene

RAS is a GTP-binding signal transducer coupled to growth-factor receptors. In its inactive state it binds GDP. On ligand binding → GDP replaced by GTP → activates downstream MAPK. RAS then hydrolyzes its own GTP (with help from GTPase-activating protein) and returns to inactive.

Oncogenic mutation (classic codons 12, 13, 61) blocks GAP interaction → RAS locked in GTP-bound active state → continuous growth signals.

Tumor Suppressor Genes — The Two-Hit Rule

Tumor suppressors restrain growth. By Knudson's two-hit hypothesis, both alleles must be inactivated for tumor formation. The "first hit" can be inherited (→ hereditary cancer syndrome, younger onset, bilateral/multifocal) or somatic (→ sporadic cancer, older onset, unilateral).

p53 — "Guardian of the Genome"

  • Regulates G1 → S checkpoint. Senses DNA damage.
  • Halts the cycle to allow repair; if repair fails → induces apoptosis via BAX (disrupts Bcl-2 → cytochrome c release → caspase cascade).
  • Lost in > 50% of human cancers.
  • Germline mutation → Li-Fraumeni syndrome — predisposition to multiple cancers (sarcoma, breast, leukemia, adrenal, brain) at young ages.

Rb — "Brake on the cell cycle"

  • Normally binds and sequesters E2F, preventing G1 → S transition.
  • Cyclin D / CDK4 phosphorylates Rb → Rb releases E2F → cell advances into S phase.
  • Mutated Rb → E2F is permanently free → unchecked proliferation.
  • Sporadic Rb mutation → unilateral retinoblastoma. Germline mutation → bilateral retinoblastoma + osteosarcoma.

Regulators of Apoptosis — Bcl-2

Bcl-2 is normally anti-apoptotic — it stabilizes the mitochondrial outer membrane, blocking cytochrome c release. In germinal-center B cells that generate an auto-reactive antibody during somatic hypermutation, failure of Bcl-2 suppression normally triggers apoptosis.

In follicular lymphoma, t(14;18) moves BCL2 (chr 18) to the IgH locus (chr 14) → Bcl-2 overexpression → mitochondrial membrane locked down → B cells that should have died survive → lymphoma.

Other Hallmarks of Cancer

  • Telomerase upregulation — prevents telomere shortening → cellular immortality.
  • Angiogenesis — tumors secrete VEGF, FGF; bevacizumab targets VEGF.
  • Immune evasion — tumors downregulate MHC I (evade CD8⁺ T cells) or upregulate PD-L1 (dampen T cells). Checkpoint inhibitors (anti-PD-1, anti-CTLA-4) reverse this.
  • Warburg effect — tumors preferentially use aerobic glycolysis; basis of FDG-PET.
IV

Tumor Progression & Metastasis

High Yield

Progression of Epithelial Neoplasia

A classic sequence: normal → hyperplasia → dysplasia → carcinoma in situ → invasive carcinoma → metastasis. Each step marks the accumulation of mutations.

  • Dysplasia — disordered growth, nuclear atypia, architectural disarray, but still confined above the basement membrane. Reversible if stimulus removed.
  • Carcinoma in situ (CIS) — full-thickness dysplasia, still within epithelium; basement membrane intact. No metastatic potential. Irreversible (by definition).
  • Invasive carcinoma — tumor cells breach the basement membrane. Metastatic potential now present.

Invasion — Molecular Steps

  1. Loss of E-cadherin — epithelial cells detach from each other.
  2. Attachment to laminin on the basement membrane.
  3. Destruction of type IV collagen by collagenase IV (MMPs) — breaches the basement membrane.
  4. Binding to fibronectin in the ECM → local spread.
  5. Entry into vascular or lymphatic space → metastasis.
The three routes of metastasis Lymphatic → typical of carcinomas. First stop: regional draining nodes. Hematogenous → typical of sarcomas and some carcinomas: renal cell, hepatocellular, follicular thyroid, choriocarcinoma. Seeding → body cavity spread. Classic: ovarian carcinoma seeding the peritoneum ("omental caking").

Histologic Features of Malignancy

Malignant tumors are classically anaplastic (poorly differentiated). Look for:

  • Loss of polarity — disorganized growth
  • Nuclear pleomorphism — variability in nuclear size/shape
  • Hyperchromasia — dark, dense nuclei
  • High nuclear-to-cytoplasmic ratio
  • Abundant & atypical mitoses
  • Invasion through basement membrane

Cachexia

Progressive loss of fat and muscle in cancer patients, not reversed by caloric intake. Driven by TNF-α, IL-1, IL-6, IFN-γ from tumor and immune cells. Distinct from simple starvation — mediated by inflammatory cytokines and PIF (proteolysis-inducing factor).

"Except" Rules & Common Traps

  • "-oma" is not always benign — lymphoma, melanoma, glioblastoma, seminoma, hepatoma, mesothelioma are all malignant
  • Unilateral retinoblastoma = sporadic (both hits somatic); bilateral = germline (first hit inherited)
  • Anti-TNF biologics + latent TB → reactivation (screen PPD before starting)
  • AFP in pregnancy elevates with NTDs (high) and Down syndrome (low) — same marker, different contexts
  • PSA elevates in prostate cancer, BPH, and prostatitis; free PSA is low in cancer
  • MEN2A: Medullary thyroid + Pheo + Parathyroid hyperplasia (RET)
  • MEN2B: Medullary thyroid + Pheo + Mucosal neuromas + Marfanoid habitus (RET)
  • MEN1: Pituitary + Parathyroid + Pancreatic (gastrinoma, insulinoma) — menin, not RET
  • Staging > grading for prognosis; M (metastasis) is the single most important factor
  • Asbestos → lung cancer > mesothelioma (most commonly); smoking is synergistic
  • Lymphatic spread → carcinomas; hematogenous → sarcomas + a few carcinomas (RCC, HCC, follicular thyroid, choriocarcinoma)
  • Cachexia is not reversed by feeding — it's cytokine-driven (TNF, IL-1, IL-6, IFN-γ)
  • HPV 6 & 11 = low-risk (condyloma acuminata, laryngeal papilloma); 16 & 18 = high-risk (cancer)
