I

Growth Adaptations

High Yield

An organ is in homeostasis with the physiologic stress placed on it. When that stress increases, decreases, or changes, the organ adapts — or fails to.

Hyperplasia vs. Hypertrophy

Hyperplasia = increase in cell number (via stem cells). Hypertrophy = increase in cell size (gene activation → more protein & organelles). They usually occur together (e.g., pregnant uterus).

⚠ Permanent Tissues Exception Cardiac muscle, skeletal muscle, and neurons cannot divide — they undergo hypertrophy only. A hypertensive patient's LV gets thicker, not more numerous, myocytes.
★ Board Buzz Pathologic hyperplasia → dysplasia → cancer (e.g., endometrial hyperplasia → endometrial carcinoma). Key exception: BPH does NOT increase prostate cancer risk.

Atrophy

Decrease in organ size via ↓ cell size AND ↓ cell number. Triggered by decreased hormonal stimulation, disuse, ischemia, or loss of innervation.

  • ↓ Cell number — apoptosis
  • ↓ Cell size — two mechanisms you must memorize:
    • Ubiquitin-proteasome degradation of intermediate filaments
    • Autophagy — autophagic vacuoles fuse with lysosomes

Metaplasia

A change in stress → change in cell type (reversible, via stem-cell reprogramming). Most commonly one type of surface epithelium replacing another.

Dysplasia, Aplasia, Hypoplasia

  • Dysplasia — disordered growth; most often precancerous (e.g., CIN → cervical cancer). Reversible if stress removed; if not → carcinoma (irreversible).
  • Aplasia — failure of organ production in embryogenesis (e.g., unilateral renal agenesis).
  • Hypoplasia — decreased organ production → small organ (e.g., streak ovary in Turner syndrome).
◉ Big Picture Normal → Hyperplasia/Hypertrophy/Metaplasia → Dysplasia → Carcinoma. Everything up through dysplasia is reversible; once cells become cancerous, there's no going back.

II

Cellular Injury

High Yield

Injury happens when stress exceeds the cell's ability to adapt. The vulnerability depends on cell type — neurons are most susceptible to ischemia; skeletal muscle is relatively resistant. Slow ischemia (renal artery atherosclerosis) causes atrophy; acute ischemia (renal artery embolus) causes injury.

Hypoxia — the central cause

Low O₂ delivery impairs oxidative phosphorylation → ↓ ATP → injury. Hypoxia has three flavors:

★ Board Buzzwords Cherry-red skin → CO poisoning. Chocolate-brown blood / cyanosis unresponsive to O₂ → methemoglobinemia, treat with IV methylene blue (reduces Fe³⁺ → Fe²⁺).

Reversible vs. Irreversible Injury

Low ATP disrupts three pumps/processes:

  1. Na⁺/K⁺ pump fails → Na & H₂O accumulate intracellularly → cellular swelling (hallmark of reversible injury)
  2. Ca²⁺ pump fails → Ca²⁺ builds in cytosol → activates destructive enzymes
  3. Switch to anaerobic glycolysis → lactic acid → ↓ pH → denatures proteins, precipitates DNA
Fig 1.2 Reversible vs. irreversible injury — key morphologic landmarks.
⚕ Classic Vignette Post-MI patient: rising troponin = myocyte plasma membrane damage (irreversible). Mitochondrial swelling alone would still be reversible — it's only rupture and cytochrome-c leak that commit the cell to death.
III

Cell Death — Necrosis

High Yield

The morphologic hallmark of cell death is loss of the nucleus, which happens in three stages:

  • Pyknosis — nuclear condensation
  • Karyorrhexis — nuclear fragmentation
  • Karyolysis — nuclear dissolution

Necrosis = death of large groups of cells followed by acute inflammation; always pathologic. Classified by gross features.

TypeGross / HistologyMechanismClassic Cause
CoagulativeFirm, wedge-shaped, pale; cell outlines preserved, nucleus goneProtein coagulation from ischemiaInfarct of any organ except brain
LiquefactiveLiquefied tissue → cystic cavityEnzymatic lysis of cellsBrain infarct · abscess · pancreatitis
GangrenousDry: mummified, black (coagulative) · Wet: liquefactive w/ infectionIschemia ± bacterial superinfectionLower limb ischemia, GI tract
CaseousSoft, friable, "cottage cheese" within granulomasCoagulative + liquefactiveTB, systemic fungi
FatChalky-white deposits (saponification)Lipase + free fatty acids + Ca²⁺Acute pancreatitis, breast trauma
FibrinoidVessel wall with bright pink proteinaceous depositsProtein leak (incl. fibrin) into wallMalignant HTN, vasculitis, preeclampsia
★ Calcification Distinction Dystrophic — Ca²⁺ deposits on dead tissue; serum Ca/PO₄ is normal (e.g., saponification in fat necrosis, atherosclerotic plaque, TB granuloma).
Metastatic — high serum Ca/PO₄ deposits in normal tissue (e.g., hyperparathyroidism → nephrocalcinosis, milk-alkali syndrome).

Apoptosis

ATP-dependent, programmed single-cell death. No inflammation. Physiologic examples: endometrial shedding, embryogenesis, CD8⁺ killing of virus-infected cells.

Morphology

  • Cell shrinks (cytoplasm becomes more eosinophilic / pink)
  • Nucleus condenses & fragments in an organized manner
  • Apoptotic bodies bud off → phagocytosed by macrophages → no inflammation

Mechanism — caspases

All three pathways converge on caspases, which activate proteases (break cytoskeleton) and endonucleases (fragment DNA).

Fig 1.3 Three pathways to apoptosis — all converge on caspases.
◉ Necrosis vs. Apoptosis Necrosis: many cells · swollen · membrane ruptured · ATP-independent · inflammation present · always pathologic.
Apoptosis: single cells · shrunken · membrane intact · ATP-dependent · no inflammation · can be physiologic.
IV

Free Radical Injury

High Yield

Free radicals = chemical species with an unpaired electron. Physiologically generated during oxidative phosphorylation (partial reduction of O₂ yields superoxide O₂⁻, H₂O₂, and hydroxyl ·OH).

Pathologic generation

  • Ionizing radiation → water hydrolyzed to hydroxyl radical
  • Inflammation → NADPH oxidase generates superoxide (neutrophil respiratory burst)
  • Metals (Fe²⁺, Cu) → generate ·OH via Fenton reaction
  • Drugs/chemicals → P450 system (e.g., acetaminophen, CCl₄)

Damage mechanisms

Free radicals cause: lipid peroxidation of membranes, protein oxidation, and DNA damage (implicated in aging & oncogenesis).

Elimination — memorize the enzymes & their locations

Classic Free-Radical Vignettes

  • Carbon tetrachloride (CCl₄) — dry-cleaning solvent → P450 converts to CCl₃· → RER swells → ribosomes detach → ↓ apolipoproteins → fatty change in liver.
  • Reperfusion injury — reintroduction of O₂ to ischemic tissue generates O₂-derived radicals. Explains continued rise in troponin after reperfusion of an MI.
  • Acetaminophen toxicity — NAPQI depletes glutathione → hepatic free radicals. Antidote: N-acetylcysteine (regenerates GSH).

Amyloidosis

High Yield

Amyloid = misfolded protein deposited in the extracellular space, damaging tissues. All amyloids share:

  • β-pleated sheet configuration
  • Congo red staining with apple-green birefringence under polarized light

"Except" Rules — Don't Fall For These

  • Pathologic hyperplasia → dysplasia → cancer — EXCEPT BPH (no prostate cancer risk)
  • Metaplasia → dysplasia → cancer — EXCEPT apocrine metaplasia of the breast
  • Coagulative necrosis happens with ischemia of every organ — EXCEPT the brain (liquefactive)
  • Hypoxemia gives low PaO₂ — EXCEPT anemia, CO poisoning, and methemoglobinemia (PaO₂ is normal)
  • Hyperplasia + hypertrophy occur together — EXCEPT in permanent tissues (heart, skeletal muscle, neurons) which do hypertrophy only
  • Necrosis causes inflammation — EXCEPT apoptosis (no inflammation)
